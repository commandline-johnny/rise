The Big Idea

You don't have to bet on
stocks going up.

Most people think investing means picking the right stock and hoping it goes up. That's one way. It's also the hardest way — and the riskiest.

Shane does something different. He collects small amounts of money, every day, by selling a kind of financial insurance policy. Whether the market goes up, down, or sideways — he usually profits.

Think of it like owning a vending machine. It doesn't matter what's happening in the world — people put money in, you collect it, every single day. Small, frequent, reliable bites of the apple.

The Stock Market

Forget everything
you think you know.

The stock market is just a giant auction that runs every weekday. The most famous index — tracking hundreds of companies at once — is called SPX.

Think of SPX like a thermometer for the US economy. Up when things are good. Down when things are bad. Bouncing around in place when nothing dramatic is happening.

Shane doesn't bet on which direction SPX will go. He bets that it won't go too far in either direction on any given day. And most days, he's right.

What Is an Option — Part 1

You've already used one.
You just didn't call it that.

You find a house you love. You're not ready to buy, but you don't want someone else to snatch it. So you give the seller $500 and say:

"I want the right to buy this house at $300,000 — but I need 30 days to decide."

That $500? That's the option premium. The seller keeps it no matter what you decide.

Shane is the seller.

Every day, he collects the $500. Whether the buyer acts or not — he keeps it.

What Is an Option — Part 2

Same idea.
Different asset.

The House Version

Buyer pays $500 for the right to buy the house at a set price within 30 days.

Seller keeps the $500 no matter what happens.

The Market Version

Buyer pays a premium for the right to "cash out" at a certain SPX price by end of day.

As time passes and SPX doesn't reach that price, the option loses value fast. Shane buys it back for far less than he sold it for — and keeps the difference.

He sells these options every trading day, then buys them back once enough value has decayed — pocketing the difference as profit.

Why Today's Options?

Time is money.
Literally.

The Ice Cube

Every option loses value as time passes — like an ice cube melting. The closer to expiration, the faster it melts.

By end of day, a 0-DTE option that didn't pay off is worth exactly zero.

Why Shane Wins

He sold those ice cubes this morning.

Every hour the market doesn't move dramatically, his options lose value — and he buys them back for less than he sold them for. The difference is his profit.

He doesn't need to be right. He just needs time to pass.

The Foundation

Two things.
That's it.

1. Defined Risk

Before Shane enters a trade, he already knows the worst thing that can happen. It's a number. It doesn't change. The market can't surprise him with a loss bigger than what he already planned for.

Most people lose money because they didn't know how bad "bad" could get. Shane always knows.

2. The Daily Goal

Once Shane hits his target for the day — he stops. Full stop. Closes the laptop, goes outside, done.

This sounds simple. It isn't. The temptation to make more is where most people blow up their accounts. The discipline to stop is the strategy.

The Hardest Part

The math is easy.
The feelings are not.

You are not deciding whether to lose money during the trade.
That decision was already made — before you entered.
  • When Shane sets his risk parameters, past-Shane says "I'm okay losing up to X today." Present-Shane doesn't re-open that debate. He just executes the plan.
  • The people this fails for aren't bad at math. They're people who can't separate their feelings from their decisions in the moment. That's human. It's why this must be learned slowly, with small amounts, until the discipline becomes automatic.

The Entry Point

You can start
with $500.

The same strategy works at any account size. With $500, you trade a smaller version of the same instrument. You set a smaller daily goal. You use narrower safety nets.

The mechanics are identical. The profit is smaller. The losses are smaller. The learning is real.

The point isn't to get rich on $500. The point is to learn the system with money you can afford to lose, build consistency, and scale up from there.

Consistent beats lucky. Every time.
