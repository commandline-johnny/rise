# 0-DTE Iron Butterfly Playbook

A daily income strategy trading zero-days-to-expiration index options.
Consistently executable. Defined risk. 80%+ win rate when followed with discipline.

---

## What This Is

An **iron butterfly** on a 0-DTE (zero days to expiration) index option — SPX or XSP depending on account size.

An iron butterfly is an iron condor where both short strikes are placed at the same price (ATM). You're selling a call vertical and a put vertical with the same short strike, collecting premium from both sides simultaneously.

The core mechanic: **you're selling time**. 0-DTE options lose value extremely fast as the day progresses. You sell premium in the morning and buy it back for less once enough has decayed — pocketing the difference. Active management protects you when the market moves against one side.

---

## Why It Works

- 0-DTE options decay extremely fast — theta (time decay) is working for you every minute the trade is open
- SPX/XSP options are cash-settled and European-style (no early assignment risk)
- Defined risk: your max loss is fixed at entry. You can never lose more than the width of your wings minus the credit you collected
- The market goes sideways more often than it makes dramatic moves
- Active management (closing one wing early) lets you recover from adverse moves most of the time

## The Trade Structure

### Instrument
- **SPX** (S&P 500 Index options) — the full-size version
- **XSP** (Mini-SPX) — identical structure, 1/10th the size, better for smaller accounts

### Structure: Iron Butterfly
- **Sell** ATM call
- **Sell** ATM put (same strike as the call)
- **Buy** call 25 points above (your upside protection)
- **Buy** put 25 points below (your downside protection)

### Wing Width
**25 points is the default.** This is your most important risk dial.

| Wing Width | Max Risk | Premium Collected | Risk Level |
|------------|----------|-------------------|------------|
| 10 pts | Lower | Less | Conservative |
| 25 pts | Medium | More | Moderate |
| 50 pts | Higher | Most | Aggressive |

Wider wings = more premium collected = more profit potential = more you can lose.
**Start narrow. Widen as your account and confidence grow.**

### Typical Credit (SPX, 25pt wings)
- Each short option: ~$12–15
- Total credit per butterfly: ~$4,000
- Target daily profit: $500–900 (after scaling up)

## Daily Routine

### Morning Read (9:00–9:30 AM CT)

1. **Scan the news** — headlines since last night. Look for anything that could cause a dramatic move: Fed announcements, CPI data, geopolitical events, earnings surprises
2. **Check /CL (crude oil futures)** — trending up, down, or sideways?
3. **Check SPX chart** — trending up, down, or sideways?
4. Cross-reference: what is oil doing vs. the market? Do they agree?

Your goal is a general feel for the day — not a precise prediction, just enough to know whether to expect a smooth or volatile session.

### Entry

- **Earliest entry**: after 9:30 AM CT (market open)
- **Preferred entry**: 9:30–10:30 AM CT on smooth trending days
- **Latest acceptable entry**: Noon CT
  - Noon is when SPX is most active (jumping around). You can enter then, but prefer smoother conditions
- **Strike placement**: ATM — whatever price SPX is trading at when you enter
- **Do not enter after 12:00 PM CT on volatile days**

### What You're Doing While You Wait

You're buying time. While your butterfly is open, theta decay is working in your favor every minute. The longer the market holds near your short strike, the cheaper it becomes to buy your position back. Your profit is the difference between what you sold the premium for and what you pay to close it. You're reading the tape while that gap widens.

## Trade Management

### Scenario 1: Market Goes Sideways

The best case. Do nothing. Watch the premium decay. Close the entire butterfly when you hit your **daily profit goal**.

### Scenario 2: Market Moves Against One Side

1. Watch SPX on **1-minute and 5-minute candle charts**
2. **Warning signal**: SPX moves ~10 points toward one of your long strikes AND is holding or continuing in that direction
3. **Action**: Close the losing wing — buy back that vertical (the entire call spread or put spread, both legs)
4. Leave the winning wing open — it has gained value and will offset your loss
5. Monitor the remaining wing and close it at your profit goal or before 2 PM

### Scenario 3: Market Moves Hard and Fast

If SPX is moving aggressively and you're approaching your long strike, close the losing wing immediately — don't wait for the 10-point rule. Cut the loss, let the winner run.

## Position Sizing: The 5% Rule

**Risk no more than 5% of your total account value per day.**

This is how you decide how many contracts to trade and how wide your wings should be.

| Account Size | 5% Daily Risk | Suggested Setup |
|-------------|---------------|-----------------|

Your max loss on any butterfly = (wing width × $100 × contracts) − credit received.
Make sure that number stays at or under your 5% limit.

## Emotional Discipline

This is where most people fail. Not the math, not the setup — the emotions.

**The core reframe:**

> *You are not deciding whether to lose money during the trade. That decision was already made when you set your risk parameters before you entered.*

When the market moves against you, your brain will want to treat it as a live decision — "should I close now? should I wait? what if it comes back?" That's the wrong frame. Past-you already decided the maximum loss was acceptable. Present-you just executes the plan.

**On a bad day:**
- Remind yourself: past me was okay losing this amount
- Remind yourself: I can afford many more bites at the apple
- Your job is not to avoid loss — it's to execute the plan within defined limits

**What emotional failure looks like:**
- Holding a losing wing too long, hoping it reverses
- Chasing profit after hitting your daily goal
- Entering on a chaotic news day because you feel like you're missing out
- Sizing up because yesterday was a good day

**What emotional discipline looks like:**
- Closing the losing wing when the signal says to, even if it hurts
- Logging off the moment you hit your profit goal
- Sitting on your hands on days that don't feel right
- Accepting a loss day as the cost of doing business

This is what "don't risk what you can't afford to lose" looks like in practice. The loss was already priced in before the trade opened.

1. **Wings define your max loss** — you can never lose more than wing width × $100 × contracts − credit received
