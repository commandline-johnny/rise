# How Shane Makes Money Every Day
### A plain-english explanation for people who thought the stock market was not for them

---

## Slide 1: The Big Idea

You don't have to bet on stocks going up.

Most people think investing means picking the right stock and hoping it goes up. That's one way. It's also the hardest way — and the riskiest.

Shane does something different. He collects small amounts of money, every day, by selling a kind of financial insurance policy. Whether the market goes up, down, or sideways — he usually profits.

This is not a get-rich-quick scheme. It's more like owning a vending machine. It doesn't matter what's happening in the world — people put in money, you collect it, every single day.

---

## Slide 2: What Is the Stock Market, Actually?

Forget everything you think you know.

The stock market is just a giant auction that runs every weekday. People buy and sell tiny pieces of companies all day long. The price of those pieces goes up and down based on what people think the company is worth.

There's also a thing called an **index** — instead of tracking one company, it tracks the average of hundreds of them at once. The most famous one is called the **S&P 500**, and it goes by the name **SPX**.

Think of SPX like a thermometer for the entire US economy. When things are good, it goes up. When things are bad, it goes down. When nothing dramatic is happening, it bounces around in place.

Shane doesn't bet on which direction SPX will go. He bets that it won't go too far in either direction on any given day. And most days, he's right.

## Slide 3: What Is an Option? (Part 1 — The Real Life Version)

You've already used one. You just didn't call it that.

Imagine you find a house you love. You're not ready to buy it yet, but you're scared someone else will buy it first. So you give the seller $500 and say:

> *"I want the right to buy this house at $300,000 — but I need 30 days to decide."*

The seller agrees. Now you have 30 days to make up your mind. If you decide to buy, great — the $500 applies. If you walk away, the seller keeps the $500.

That $500? That's the **option premium**. You paid for the right to make a decision later, without being forced to act now.

The seller took your $500 and said "sure, I'll wait." They collected the premium. Whether you buy or not, they keep it.

**Shane is the seller.**

---

## Slide 4: What Is an Option? (Part 2 — The Market Version)

Same idea, but instead of a house, it's the S&P 500.

In the market, you can buy and sell options on SPX. These options give the buyer the right to "cash out" at a certain price by a certain time.

The buyer pays a **premium** — a small amount of money — for that right.

If SPX doesn't move to where the buyer needs it to go, their option expires worthless. They lose their premium.

The seller — the person who collected the premium — keeps it. No matter what.

Shane sells these options. Every day. He collects the premium in the morning. Most days, the options expire worthless and he keeps all of it. On days the market moves too much, he has a plan for that too.

---

## Slide 5: The Shape of the Trade

Shane doesn't just sell one option. He sells four at once, arranged in a specific shape called an **iron butterfly**.

Here's what that looks like without any jargon:

Imagine SPX is at 6,800 right now.

- Shane bets it won't go above 6,825 today → he collects money for that bet
- Shane bets it won't go below 6,775 today → he collects money for that bet too
- But he also buys protection in case he's wrong → he pays a small amount for insurance at 6,850 and 6,750

So he's collecting premium from two bets, and paying a small amount for two safety nets.

**If SPX stays between 6,775 and 6,825 today → he wins both bets and keeps the premium.**

**If SPX moves past one of his bets → his safety net kicks in and limits his loss.**

He can never lose more than the distance between his bet and his safety net. The maximum loss is locked in before the trade even opens.

---

## Slide 6: Why Today? (The 0-DTE Part)

Why does Shane only use options that expire the same day?

Because time is money — literally.

Every option has an expiration date. The closer you get to that date, the faster the option loses value. By the end of the day, a 0-DTE option is worth exactly zero if the bet didn't pay off.

This means Shane's options are melting like ice cubes from the moment he sells them. Every hour that passes without a dramatic market move, his options lose value — and that's good for him, because he sold them.

He doesn't need to be right about which direction the market goes. He just needs the market to not move too far. And time does the rest.

## Slide 7: A Typical Morning

Here's what Shane actually does:

**9:00–9:30 AM:** Read the news. Check oil prices. Look at what the market is doing. Get a general feel — is today going to be calm or chaotic?

**9:30–10:30 AM:** Place the trade. Sell the iron butterfly right at wherever SPX is sitting. Collect the premium.

**Then:** Watch. Wait. Let time work.

If the market drifts sideways, he does nothing. He watches the premium melt away and closes everything when he's hit his profit goal for the day.

If the market starts moving toward one of his safety nets, he acts — closes the losing side early and lets the winning side run to cover it.

**By noon, ideally before 2 PM at the latest: he's done.**

## Slide 8: The Two Things That Make This Work

**1. Defined risk**

Before Shane enters a trade, he already knows the worst thing that can happen. It's a number. It doesn't change. The market can't surprise him with a loss bigger than what he already planned for.

Most people who lose money in the market lose it because they didn't know how bad "bad" could get. Shane always knows.

**2. The daily profit goal**

Once Shane hits his target for the day — he stops. Full stop. Closes the laptop, goes outside, done.

This sounds simple. It isn't. The temptation to make more is where most people blow up their accounts. The discipline to stop is what makes the strategy work over hundreds of trades, not just a lucky streak.

## Slide 9: What About Bad Days?

They happen. Here's what one looked like.

**April 6, 2026.** The market went absolutely haywire because of a geopolitical crisis. SPX made a fast, dramatic move the moment Shane was in his trade. There was no waiting — the move was too big, too fast.

Shane's goal changed immediately: *forget profit. Minimize the damage.*

He closed the losing side as fast as he could. He took the loss.

**Result:** He lost about $230 on a $37,000 account. Less than 1%.

The next day he made $540. The day after, $690. The week recovered completely.

The strategy didn't fail. It worked exactly as designed — it just returned a small, contained loss instead of a profit. And he lived to trade another day.

## Slide 10: The Emotional Part (The Hardest Part)

The math is easy. The feelings are not.

When the market moves against you, your brain panics. It starts asking: *should I close now? should I wait? what if it comes back?*

Here's the reframe that makes this manageable:

> **You are not deciding whether to lose money during the trade. That decision was already made — before you entered.**

When Shane sets his risk parameters, past-Shane says "I'm okay losing up to X today." When the market moves, present-Shane doesn't re-open that debate. He just executes the plan.

This is hard. It goes against every instinct. It's also the entire game.

The people this strategy won't work for aren't bad at math. They're people who can't separate their feelings from their decisions in the moment. That's not a flaw — it's human. But it's why this has to be learned slowly, with small amounts of money, until the discipline becomes automatic.

## Slide 12: You Can Start With $500

The exact same strategy works at any account size.

With $500, you trade a smaller version of the same instrument (XSP instead of SPX). You set a smaller daily goal ($200). You use narrower safety nets.

The mechanics are identical. The profit is smaller. The losses are smaller. The learning is real.

The point isn't to get rich on $500. The point is to learn the system with money you can afford to lose, build consistency, and scale up from there.

**Consistent beats lucky. Every time.**
